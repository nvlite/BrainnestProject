WORDS & THEIR ETYMOLOGIES

How can so much meaning be crammed into these abstract symbols we call letters.

Cornucopia (countable and uncountable, plural cornucopias) (Greek mythology)

  1. a curved, hollow goat's horn or similarly shaped receptacle (such as a horn-shaped basket) that is overflowing especially with fruit and vegetables (such as gourds, ears of corn, apples, and grapes) and that is used as a decorative motif emblematic of abundance called also horn of plenty
  2. an inexhaustible store: ABUNDANCE
  3. a receptacle shaped like a horn or cone

Halcyon, hal·​cy·​on ˈhal-sē-ən.

  1. characterized by happiness, great success, and prosperity : golden. often used to describe an idyllic time in the past that is remembered as better than today
  2. calm, peaceful
  3. prosperous, Affluent
  4. a bird identified with the kingfisher and held in ancient legend to nest at sea about the time of the winter solstice and to calm the waves during incubation

    5. The word halcyon comes from a story in Greek mythology about the halcyon bird, which had the power to calm the rough ocean waves every December so she could nest. In Greece these days come around early January

Balkanize

  1. to divide (a country, territory, etc.) into small, quarrelsome, ineffectual states.
  2. (often lowercase) to divide (groups, areas, etc.) into contending and usually ineffectual factions:

    3. "ambitious neighbours would snatch pieces of territory, Balkanizing the country"

Goblin Mode

  1. Goblin mode is a neologism for the rejection of societal expectations and the act of living in an unkempt, hedonistic manner without concern for one's self-image.

    2. Here is a quote from the Smithsonian website:

    For the first time in history, Oxford Dictionaries allowed the public to vote on its 2022 word of the year. The winner: “goblin mode.”

Sabotage

  1. destruction of an employer's property (such as tools or materials) or the hindering of manufacturing by discontented workers
  2. destructive or obstructive action carried on by a civilian or enemy agent to hinder a nation's war effort
  3. an act or process tending to hamper or hurt
  4. deliberate subversion

    5. One theory related to the invention of the Jacquard loom by Frenchman Joseph Marie Jacquard in 1801. The Jacquard loom uses punch cards to automate the raising and lowering of warp threads, allowing one to achieve many more sheds than a human weaver could ever grapple with. This invention, however, put many textile workers out of a job. The theory is that these employees threw their wooden clogs (the French word for these clogs is “sabots”) into the delicate machinery to wreak havoc.
